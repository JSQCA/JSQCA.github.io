The Bergen Arches is a mile-long, below-grade rail corridor running through the heart of Ward C — and it's on its way to becoming one of Jersey City's most significant green spaces. The JSQCA has been an active supporter of this project since its early days.

What Are the Bergen Arches?

The Bergen Arches (formally the "Erie Cut") is an abandoned four-track railroad trench blasted through Bergen Hill between 1906 and 1910 by the Erie Railroad. The cut runs roughly from Palisade Avenue (near the Heights) to Tonnelle Avenue/Route 1&9, passing between the Journal Square and Heights neighborhoods.

Six arched bridges and tunnels were built along its length to keep intersecting streets intact — giving the corridor its name. At its deepest, the trench sits 40 to 85 feet below street level and spans approximately 60 feet wide, covering about 17 acres.

The last passenger train passed through in 1957. Over more than 60 years of abandonment, the cut has grown into a dense urban forest — one of the most biodiverse ecosystems in the area.

The Vision

The plan is to transform the Bergen Arches into a shared-use greenway and transit corridor — a linear park with pedestrian and cycling trails, preserved natural habitat, and adaptively reused historic tunnels. The project is frequently compared to New York City's High Line.

Key elements of the vision include:

  • A pedestrian and cycling trail running the full length of the corridor
  • Preservation of the established urban forest and ecology
  • Adaptive reuse of historic tunnels for community, cultural, and educational programming
  • Five equitably distributed access points along the corridor
  • Potential integration with sustainable public transit
  • No vehicular traffic

The Bergen Arches would also serve as a critical link connecting the East Coast Greenway (a national trail from Maine to Florida), the Essex-Hudson Greenway (a 9-mile car-free pathway connecting 8 communities from Montclair to Jersey City), and the Sixth Street Embankment greenway toward the Jersey City waterfront.

Current Status

The Bergen Arches Greenway & Transit Feasibility Study, commissioned by Jersey City's Department of Infrastructure and conducted by Supermass Studio, Arup, and NHDM, was completed in 2024. It was funded by a $100,000 state grant and won a 2025 ASLA-NY Honor Award. The full study is available at the Bergen Arches Feasibility Study Hub.

In 2025, NJ Transit announced a $22.28 million Phase 2 design contract for a Bus Rapid Transit Transitway from Secaucus to Jersey City that would use the Bergen Arches corridor. Community advocates, including BAPC, are pushing for coexistence of greenway and transit uses within the corridor.

Upcoming Milestones

  • 2024–2025: Phase I Environmental Site Assessment (NJEDA Brownfields program)
  • 2025: Site Investigation / Remedial Investigation; NJ Transit Transitway Master Plan Study
  • Late 2027: JFK Boulevard & Route 139 viaduct replacement (estimated)
  • Summer 2030: Palisade Avenue bridge replacement (estimated)

Key Facts

  • Length: ~1 mile (corridor extends ~1.75 miles with adjacent right-of-way)
  • Depth: 40–85 feet below street level
  • Area: ~17 acres
  • Built: 1906–1910 by the Erie Railroad
  • Last train: 1957
  • Current owner: NJ Transit (primary), Conrail, NJDOT
  • Feasibility study: Completed 2024, won 2025 ASLA-NY Honor Award

Key Organizations
